*By Krisha & Mohammed*

As global political tensions begin to reshape everyday life and India openly supports Israel in an international conflict, Iran retaliates by blocking Indian ships passing through the Strait of Hormuz. *(An almost impossible future for the most of us to image, I am sure \- but try nonetheless, it might just happen).*

Since a large portion of India’s oil imports travel through this route, the disruption causes a sudden spike in petrol prices. The rising fuel costs trigger massive inflation across the country. Transportation, manufacturing, and electricity prices surge. **However, the most severe impact appears in agriculture.**

Indian agriculture depends heavily on nitrogen and phosphate-based fertilizers to maintain crop yields. With global supply chains disrupted and fertilizer prices begin skyrocketing, food production begins to decline. **This leads to rising food prices and a growing fear of food shortages.**

Large agricultural corporations start searching desperately for alternative sources of nitrogen.

This desperate search begins to leak inwards and ends at the bronchia of auto-rickshaw drivers. Workers who spend years in heavily polluted urban environments such as auto‑rickshaw drivers, delivery riders, and bike taxi drivers have unusually high accumulations of nitrogen compounds in their lungs due to long-term exposure to vehicle emissions like nitrogen dioxide (NO₂).

Seeing a business opportunity, biotech companies develop a controversial method to extract nitrogen compounds from human lung tissue after death and convert them into fertilizer components.

**Soon, corporations launch a new type of insurance-like contract targeted at people working in polluted environments.**

Sales agents go door-to-door in neighborhoods where drivers and delivery workers live. The offer is simple: **sign a contract allowing the company to collect your lungs after death. In return, your family will valuable receive compensation.**

The payment depends on how much nitrogen can be extracted from the lungs **the more damage caused by years of polluted air, the higher the payout.**

For many struggling families facing rising inflation and uncertain futures, the offer becomes difficult to refuse.

Over time, polluted air transforms from a public health crisis into a new economic resource. (This is called progress). Society begins to question whether the system is helping the poor or quietly turning their suffering into profit \- in either case, we no longer worry about AQI, just the insurance payouts we are all likely to receive from our degraded lungs.
