*By Juiee*

In this speculative future, algorithms are legally classified as **perishable infrastructure.**

Like food, medicine, or industrial chemicals, every algorithm carries a manufacturing date, shelf life, storage conditions, and regulated disposal protocol. Recommendation systems, predictive models, ranking engines, and automated decision systems are treated not as invisible digital tools but as industrial products that shape public environments.

Because algorithms influence emotions, markets, migration systems, financial decisions, and political discourse, their effects are treated as public conditions rather than purely private experiences. Their stability must therefore be monitored.

Each year, the **Attention Regulation Authority** publishes an Algorithm Shelf Life and Disposal Report. Active systems are evaluated using indicators such as emotional amplification, cognitive overload, bias accumulation, and systemic risk exposure. Algorithms are classified as approved, under review, or discontinued, similar to pharmaceuticals or environmental pollutants.

*But algorithms do not simply stop working when they expire.*

***They decompose.***

Over time, algorithmic systems accumulate distortions. Data feedback loops intensify bias, recommendation engines push increasingly extreme emotional triggers, and prediction systems reinforce unstable patterns. These accumulated distortions are recognized as algorithmic decay.

As algorithms approach the end of their shelf life, instability begins to appear across the devices and systems that run them. Interfaces behave erratically, content streams oscillate between emotional extremes, files fragment, and systems slow down. Devices running expired algorithms exhibit material signs of degradation, making algorithmic decay physically perceptible rather than abstract.

Once an algorithm crosses its expiry threshold, its certification is revoked and it enters a regulated disposal pipeline. Expired logic is transferred to **Algorithm Dustbin Facilities,** fragmented into smaller code components, then archived, neutralized, or permanently stored in **Algorithm Landfills,** where obsolete computational systems accumulate as infrastructural waste.

Corporations operating thousands of algorithms must manage the logistical and environmental cost of disposal. Improper dumping becomes a regulatory violation, and companies may attempt to offload near expired systems into regions with weaker oversight, creating algorithm waste zones.

**Algorithm lifespans also vary geographically** due to regulatory climates, data environments, and infrastructural stability. When people travel, devices may carry algorithms already expired under local regulatory standards. Border systems detect the mismatch and activate a translation layer that temporarily converts the foreign algorithm into a locally certified version. If translation fails, the algorithm is safely deactivated.

*People remain constant. Algorithms must continually justify their right to exist.*
