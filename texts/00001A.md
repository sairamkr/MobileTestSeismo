*by Nikhil*

*Democracy is delicate.** You are usually pushed toward a choice: do the hard work of getting informed, of cutting through propaganda, understanding how institutions function, learning what parties actually stand for; or ignore all that and vote on faith in whoever seems trustworthy or strong!

The system rarely gives people the time, clarity, or structure to vote only once they fully understand what they're signing up for. So even when you do the work, the knowledge can seem to arrive too late, or count for too little, until "being informed" feels almost pointless.

**So what if each party's manifesto were packaged as a pill: a single dose of complete knowledge that sharpens your ability to reason and decide?**

But there is a catch, and it exposes the tension at the heart of democratic choice. If you take a party's pill, you can no longer vote for that party - because you are supposed to cast your vote with the pill itself. To know a part completely, to swallow it's pill  is to be disqualified from supporting it.

So to vote for a party, you must drop its pill, unswallowed, into the **pill-bank**  Refusing full knowledge of the very party you're choosing, and consume all the others instead, learning everything about everyone you will *not* vote for.

And if you take every pill, understand every party, every trade-off, every hidden incentive, the flood will simply overwhelm you: and though extreemly well infomrmed you will be left exhausted and unable to vote at all.

Either way the voter stays trapped between two imperfect paths. The difficult work of becoming informed, or the easy comfort of faith, inside a system that never quite lets choice be as free or equal as it claims, a paerfect evelution of our great democratic system. **Consume a pill to learn who you cannot vote for; or refuse the pill for the party you already believe in.**
