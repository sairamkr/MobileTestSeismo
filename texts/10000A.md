







#10000
*By Rushaan & Mridul*

*Humans once worked to survive.** Factories, farms, warehouses, kitchens, delivery routes, entire economies ran on human effort. Work shaped identity; it structured time, status, and purpose.

**Then machines became better at it.** Construction automated. Delivery systems drove themselves. Warehouses moved without people. Even service jobs began to vanish. Machines were faster, cheaper, tireless, and within a few decades most human labour became economically unnecessary.

Governments introduced **Universal Human Income** to stave off collapse. A guaranteed income so consumption could continue without jobs. People no longer worked to survive. But something else disappeared along with employment. **Routine went. Skill went.** Entire professions vanished within a single generation, and the gestures of labour like lifting, repairing, assembling, improvising, faded from everyday life.

Work had been replaced, but its memory had not. So people began to record it. Former workers documented their jobs before they disappeared: filming themselves performing tasks the machines had taken, telling stories of hard shifts, repetitive motions, and small techniques only experience could teach. What began as scattered clips became a centralised platform : **the Human Labour Archive**, a place to upload your former profession.

Warehouse packers show how they stacked cargo. Mechanics explain the sounds a machine made before it failed. Delivery riders map shortcuts no algorithm knew. Visitors browse professions that no longer exist. Jobs are no longer performed, they are remembered. Contributors even receive small Universal Human Income bonuses for preserving this labour heritage, and the platform presents itself as a cultural museum of human work.

**But the archive serves another purpose.**

Engineers found that human labour is rich in valuable data - hesitation, judgment, improvisation, the decisions machines struggle to replicate. These recordings train automated systems. **Machines learn how humans once worked.** The archive preserves labour history while quietly turning human experience into data.

Work no longer exists as employment. **Yet its memory continues to power the machines that replaced it.**
