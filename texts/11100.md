Excerpts from *a presentation unfolding at a popular food-delivery company:*

"Here's some food for thought. Our company now leads the world in food-delivery excellence with over 18 million active, happy monthly users! And we stand at a crucial inflection point: one where we can decisively sprint ahead of the competition.

**Data packets!** Over the last quarter we've seen the sharpest rise in data-packet prices since the birth of the internet, and we're uniquely placed to supply multiple layers of them. Serving our primary users, yes, but expanding into a vast market of secondary and tertiary users. Based on demand projections, one could even argue for a whole new *primary* user.

We can already package accurately mapped traffic, dietary preferences, disposable income, and more. But that's not where the real gold lies.

Our true advantage is a single software layer that sits quietly between the moment a user orders food and the moment it arrives. **A Verification Infrastructure.** We ask the user to upload a specific body part. Labelled. And the parts we ask for grow less private as they climb our membership tiers.

**With one elegant swing, we cement our place as the world's leading food-delivery company, while building a scalable pipeline for human metadata.**

*(Pause. Followed by applause.)*

And the profits from this new front flow straight back into unbelievable discounts and better worker conditions.

*(Even more applause.)*

Dinner arrives. Data arrives. Everyone is served.

So the question is no longer *why are we in the data business?* The question is: **why would anyone in the food business choose not to be?**"

*Presentation ends.*

The convenience economy has grown so vast that it has turned us into docile lapdogs for conglomerates to pick and pull at will. Near-frictionless consumption has us believing in magic; the gratification runs so high we simply accepted it as the new way of life. What if we were never just consumers, but complicit data farms, selling our bodies for 20% off the next dinner?
