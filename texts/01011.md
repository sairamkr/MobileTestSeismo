*By Tanmay & Milli*

Why should humans move freely across every landscape when ecosystems themselves cannot?

Every environment carries its own bacteria, microbes, and biological balances, yet human mobility ignores these invisible systems, crossing borders without adapting to the living conditions of each place.

In this world, crossing a border is no longer a political procedure but a biological one. Entry into a new territory requires the body to adapt to that ecosystem's microbial environment; if it cannot biologically coexist with the local bacteria, the border simply will not open.

The **Ecological Border Adaptation Protocol** ties human mobility directly to ecological compatibility. At every checkpoint, travellers consume a sequence of specially engineered adaptation pills, then enter **Ecological Adaptation Chambers** that simulate the biological conditions of their destination. Over **three days**, the pills gradually adjust the body to unfamiliar bacteria, microorganisms, and conditions. Only then may the traveller safely enter.

Without this preparation, human bodies risk contaminating fragile ecosystems, or falling prey to foreign microbes themselves. The border thus works not as a wall but as a **biological filter**, admitting only bodies capable of living within an ecosystem's microbial balance. Crossing becomes a waiting ritual, a pause in which humans must biologically negotiate with the land before entering it. Instead of presenting a passport alone, you must prove your body can coexist with the living systems of the place.

The speculation grows from real anxieties about ecological fragility and the invisible biological exchanges that ride along with global movement. As ecosystems grow more sensitive to disturbance, biotechnological systems begin to govern how bodies meet environments, exaggerating a question already apparent today: **what if ecosystems, not governments, held the authority to control human movement?**

By imagining borders as biological thresholds, the project challenges the assumption that humans can inhabit any landscape without consequence, and asks us to reconsider mobility not as a right granted by nations, but as a negotiation with the living systems that make environments possible.
