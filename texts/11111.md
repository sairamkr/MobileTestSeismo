When the world is a race to prosperity or doom, why waste your hours sleeping when you can **freeze that unproductive time for future use?**

**The Frozen Hours Protocol links a worker's biological clock directly to their productivity record.** Take leave, stop working, or drop out of the labour system, and your time is simply frozen. While the rest of society keeps ageing and advancing, you enjoy the luxury of suspension, for weeks OR months. Take as much as you need; it will be waiting when you return.

You return to the past, of course, all your frozen time still unspent. But don't worry: you can endure extended 24-hour shifts until your timeline catches up. No need to sleep, just swallow your frozen time in the form of our patented **Frozen Time Cubes**, each a compressed unit of eight hours, consumed in seconds.

To rest, after all, is the most unnatural thing in the world. Your productivity is no longer private; it affects the very foundations of our glorious nation. Gone is the tyranny of equal time - now we can measure it, distribute it, control it. Time belongs to those who keep working, as it rightly should. *Have you ever seen your supervisors rest? Have you ever seen your bosses stop making money? They earn even in their sleep, and now you can too.*

The Frozen Hours Protocol emerged from a need to turn a society's fallow hours into something productive. Your time does not belong to you; it belongs to that which makes you a productive member. Visionaries like Mr. Narayana Murthy foresaw this, pushing for 12- and 17- and 20-hour working days. But why stop at 24 hours, when you can reach into the past and leap into a future of endless productivity?

With labour laws already shifting in unexpected ways, perhaps we are on our way toward exactly these distortions of human worth. Freezing time only exaggerates the condition to reveal a troubling possibility: what if the right to age, progress, and exist in sync with society depended entirely on continuous labour?

**The speculation asks how our systems already discipline bodies through a marked and measured, time and how unequal access to rest hardens into broader social inequity.**
