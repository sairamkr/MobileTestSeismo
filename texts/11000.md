







#11000
*By Mrudula*

**Inter-caste marriages are so rare, so socially unsettling, that society has turned them into frozen artefacts.**

Couples who cross caste boundaries are symbolically preserved inside ice cubes in a repository. Each cube carries a distinct colour, set by the perceived "purity level" of the union, its hue determined by which castes have crossed. They resemble flavoured ice treats, visually vibrant yet socially loaded. **People approach the great ice blocks, study their colours, and even lick them to taste the transgression:** *the sweetness of love and companionship, the bitterness of stigma and judgment, and the rigid social boundaries that refuse to melt.*

The cubes turn relationships into spectacle and make the hierarchy visible. **In a society deeply invested in caste and purity, these frozen forms work as markers, preserving what is seen as transgression while warning others against crossing the same lines.**

Seen from our present moment, this world feels unsettlingly familiar. Even now, as laws and attitudes shift, ideas of purity, honour, and suitability still shape how relationships are perceived. Honour killings that violently dictate whom one may love, communities that discourage certain unions, social pressure that decides which loves are celebrated and which resisted. Many couples who challenge caste boundaries still meet emotional isolation, strained family ties, and quiet distancing: forms of symbolic freezing that halt the natural flow of a relationship.

The speculation asks us to notice how easily hierarchy embeds itself in everyday practice and tradition, until we stop questioning it. What looks aesthetic or harmless on the surface may still carry deep structures of exclusion beneath.

Perhaps, it suggests, if we turned our prejudices into material objects, we might finally ask why certain relationships are still judged through inherited notions of purity and belonging.

**Rather than offering a distant fantasy in which we solve this intractable problem, the frozen cubes reveal how long society has tried to preserve and control social order by regulating intimacy itself, through every new technology it can find.**
