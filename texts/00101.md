*By Janavi & Sarvesh*

Why should your extra hours simply vanish at the end of the day, when they could be stored and counted?

Overtime is no longer informal or unnoticed. Every extra hour can be deposited into a personal **Overtime Bank.** Offices and factories install machines that look exactly like ATMs, except they dispense nothing. They collect time. At the end of a shift, you press your finger to the sensor and the screen thanks you, politely as itsuch machines always do: *"Overtime successfully deposited." "Thank you for your contribution."* The process feels official, even rewarding, and your balance of extra hours climbs and becomes a visible, growing record of your dedication.

The **Overtime Bank Protocol** was introduced to improve productivity. The theory was simple: if overtime were tracked like money, workers would take pride in the mounting total.

But there is one rule. Once time is deposited, it **cannot be withdrawn.** (Time afterall moves linearly) Those hours cannnot return as rest, or pay, or freedom. They remain on record as proof your undyiing of loyalty. Slowly the machine fills with thousands of hours, donated by workers who must keep believing their efforts will one day count towards something greater than themselves.

Exaggerated as it sounds, it only makes visible what already exists. The late nights, the after-hours messages, the personal time surrendered without equal rest or pay. The **Overtime ATM** turns that invisible arrangement into an object you can see: labour recorded, praised, and never returned.
