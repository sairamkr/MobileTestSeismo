*By Ishan*

*EXHIBIT 07: Maharashtra State BPL Ration Card. Recovered from the Hadapsar Municipal Archive, circa 2092. National Museum of Post-Independence India, Mumbai.*

The card you're looking at is a century old.

It was issued by the Office of the Tehsildar in Pune, Maharashtra, in 2003. The person in the photograph is Abani Singh, head of a household of five, living in Galli No. 4, Dattanagar, Baragram. The card entitled him to fifteen kilograms of rice and two kilograms of sugar each month at subsidised prices. It is laminated, and bears a government seal, a card number, an entitlement table, and the signature of a state official.

By the time you read this, it is 2094. What strikes historians about this object is not what it says, but the fact that it needed to exist at all, that whether a family ate this month could depend on whether this rectangle of plastic was physically in their possession. That the state's entire relationship with a person, their identity, their need, their entitlement, could be laminated, and lost in a pocket.

Historians have arrived at a consistent interpretation. They describe it as a priority access credential: the photograph as evidence that identity verification was central to the privilege system of the era, the entitlement table as a schedule of benefits made available to verified, state-approved citizens. 
The historians are reading the object in the only language available to them - one in which a government document bearing your photograph and an official stamp means a single thing: the state chose you.

What is hidden, because the archive that would explain it has mostly rotted or been lost, is that to hold this card in 2003 was to be formally classified as poor. BPL was not a distinction the state made to honour you. It was a threshold: your household income had been measured and found insufficient, and the card was proof of that insufficiency. You carried it to a ration shop, handed it to a man behind a counter, and waited while he recorded how much of the month's allowance you had used.

Getting it required proof of address, a cooperative local official, and the patience to return to the same office week after week. Millions who were poorer still held no card at all - too mobile, too undocumented, too invisible even for this system to reach. Being seen by the state was already a privilege.

*The document of deprivation was also a document of partial inclusion.*
