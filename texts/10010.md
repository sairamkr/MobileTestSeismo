*By Aarya & Amritaa*

What is the **fetus but a transit passenger, a freeloader?**

Imagine a future where pregnancy is no longer a solitary act of bodily endurance or love, no longer burdened by silly antiquated cultural dictums. A developing fetus moves through different bodies like a *traveller between stations*. Carried briefly by one person, transferred to another, incubated in machines built to replicate the womb. **Gestation is no longer bound to a single uterus but fragmented, shared, distributed across bodies and technologies, all to optimise and speed the process.** This is not science fiction; it is the logical endpoint of technologies already here.

In this landscape of technological mediation, the fetus is an asset in transit, moving between biotech incubators and human "infrastructure." The system **financialises the womb**, converting gestational labour into tracked contributions, risk scores, and compensation tiers, an untapped source of GDP! As ever, reproductive progress benefits those with the capital to outsource risk, while marginalised bodies absorb the physical toll under the guise of economic opportunity.

We already live in a world where reproduction is technologically mediated, separated from single bodies, shaped by money and power. Most gestational workers are poor women in developing nations, carrying babies for wealthy international families and then surrendering them, often with permanent psychological consequences and zero follow-up care. **This is now.** Distributed pregnancy would only intensify what is already real: the separation of gestational labour from motherhood, the economic desperation that makes "choice" meaningless, the erasure of the people who do the work.

It forces the question we keep avoiding: if we can fragment pregnancy across bodies and machines, what does that reveal about how we value the work of gestation? About whose bodies we trust? About **what we're willing to sacrifice for "progress"?** This speculation is a mirror, and what it reflects is us — our present contradictions, our comfortable silence, our complicity in systems we claim to oppose. The future may be speculative; the questions are urgent and real. **What is consent? Whose bodies matter? What counts as labour?**
