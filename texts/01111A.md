*By Manvi Khatri* 

My neighbour has been disappearing, I have seen her edges soften and the colour leache out of her skin. 

I should have spoken her name. I know that. I should have walked over and said something. Instead I watched her dissolve into the air and I thought: someone else will notice. Someone always does. No one did.

Afterall existence is not a given \- it has to be maintained. The world does not persist on its own. It persists because we attend to it: because we watch, discuss, document, argue, share, and return our gaze to the same places and people often enough that they hold their shape.

**What no one watches long enough simply stops being.**

If it happens to people it happens to entire countries, too. There was one, I don't remember its name, which is exactly the point, that had committed the unforgivable error of being at peace for too long. No conflicts to cover, no disasters to mourn, no elections dramatic enough to trend. One year it missed the threshold entirely. The maps adjusted overnight. No one grieved, because grief requires you to remember what was lost, and by the time the borders re-drew themselves there was nothing left to remember.

Each morning I check what has materialised overnight, scrolling through what the collective gaze has chosen to sustain: which cities are vivid, which celebrities remain solid enough to cast shadows, which crises have gathered enough witnesses to fully exist. The world assembling itself fresh each day, shaped by what we find worthy of our looking.

**But attention is finite. It must be taken from somewhere and given to somewhere else. Every act of focus is also an act of neglect.** You are always, in the very moment of looking, allowing something beyond your peripheral vision to thin and fade. 

They say there was an era when this was merely a metaphor. The "attention economy" when algorithms determined what was visible and what was buried.

**Now we know that being online and being visible is to be substantial \-  making our collective focus the only force that stabilizes reality itself.** 

Sometimes I worry about the things we cannot heed \- the news we scroll past, the slow disasters we forget. I wonder what has already disappeared because we could not bear to look at it. *Famine is difficult to watch. Poverty is tedious in its repetition. Cruelty persists only because it is spectacular, while kindness is ordinary, and thins at the edges every day.*

What frightens me is not that things vanish. It is that the vanishing is so gentle. There is no violence in it. No alarm. Things simply become less, and then they are not, **and you cannot mourn what you do not remember having lost.**
