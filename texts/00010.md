*By Bhumika & Devaki*

*Wait! Are you human? Solve 8 + 13 before you continue.*

Why must you prove you're human every time you open a website? A small box appears; you pick out the bicycles, the buses, the traffic lights. You type distorted letters, drag a puzzle piece into place. These gestures feel ordinary now — we perform them without thinking, just to log in, or download a file.

**CAPTCHAs** were built to separate humans from machines, to prove the person behind the screen is real. But something strange has happened: artificial intelligence now solves these tests too, often faster than we do. If a machine can pass the test meant to catch it, the question changes. It is no longer *how do we detect machines?* but something more uncomfortable: **how do we prove we are human at all?**

This project imagines a world that measures humanity differently. Instead of tests, society tracks a subtle, invisible quality called **existential weight**. It isn't physical. It gathers in the small human moments, like when we pause, hesitate, doubt, or take time to think before acting. **These are the moments that signal presence: that a person is thinking, feeling, existing with awareness.**

The more weight you carry, the more human you appear. Your presence registers. Your thoughts feel meaningful. You are recognised, valued, seen. *Here, to be human is to be rare and precious.*

But those who move too fast begin to lose it. Instant replies, constant productivity, automatic behaviour - all make a person lighter. Without pauses, presence fades. Society overlooks them; their ideas carry less weight; their actions leave no mark. To become *less human* in this system is not merely to be ignored, it is to go weightless, to move through the world like a ghost, alive but without influence, as though nothing you do quite matters.

**And so CAPTCHA becomes salvation: we solve them endlessly to reassure ourselves that we are still here, still human, still heavy enough to be seen.**

The speculation asks how systems of efficiency are already reshaping the way we value human presence. It leaves you with a quieter question than *Are you human?*

**How much of you still is?**
