*By Janvi*

The "Great Blandness" has turned eating into a chore. The collapse of ecosystems across the oldest of our biomes like the Aravalli Hills and the Western Ghats has obliterated not just rare crops and herbs, but entire ways of cooking and culture. 

Since these ecosystems are extinct, our own taste buds have become useless, leaving us to eat tasteless fuel. To fix this, a  new industry auctions off the "restored" tongues of those who lived when the world was still lush and full of spice. 

**The tongues of the dead have become the final repositories of taste.** These tongues are preserved, cleaned, and fitted with tiny wires so they can be temporarily swapped into a living person’s mouth. **When you graft a dead person’s tongue onto your own nerves, you aren't just eating; you are accessing a biological hard drive of lost sensations. A veritable memory of the culture itself\!**

*Chewing a tasteless nutrition bar suddenly feels like tasting wild forest honey or spicy, rain-soaked herbs because the dead tongue still "remembers" those complex chemicals.* 

What is now a high-priced ritual for the wealthy, who pay millions at auctions to replace their own numb organs with these "relics" for a single meal \- might soon become accessible to all.   
The ultimate irony: after destroying the places where these flavours lived, the modern world now buys their body parts just to experience a ghost of the sweetness we erased. *We have become a civilization that survives on lab-grown science but starves for a single, stolen memory of the flavour (But hasn't that always been the way of progress?)*

Extinction is redefined not just as the disappearance of species, landscapes, or cultures, but as the total loss of sensation. In this scenario, **the human body, its memory, biology, and sensory data has become the final, marketable commodity.**

Don't we already live in a world where a combination of ecological loss and technological advancement place growing value on rare experiences. 

The deep dependence of our food cultures implicates all that lives on this planet \- and we must recognize that the choices and actions we make today directly determine what future generations will be able to taste, experience, and preserve as memory \- even that which lives momentarily on the tips of our tongues.
