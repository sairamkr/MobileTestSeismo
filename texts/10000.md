







#10000
*By Tripti & Dhruvi*

For several millennia, humans have reshaped cows through domestication and selective breeding. Wild aurochs became specialised dairy and beef breeds, built for human use. A dairy cow today gives more than four times the milk of one in the 1950s, and beef systems have been optimised for rapid growth and high yield. Their **bodies, genetics, and reproduction have been shaped to serve efficiency, mass production, and global food demand.**

 The long history of genetic manipulation and fiddling with breeding patterns has now triggered an unexpected biological response! **A global genetic mutation spreads through cattle, shrinking every cow to pocket size.** What was once a large agricultural animal becomes a fragile miniature that fits in the palm of a hand , unable to give milk in useful quantities, no longer viable for meat, its industrial value gone.

Cows begin to live in a wholly different relationship with humans: kept in small enclosures, glass cases, and protective habitats to shield them from harm, even from predators as small as rodents. As their utility vanishes, their symbolic and sacred value intensifies.

But **the last original-sized cow remains.**

Governments, dairy corporations, religious institutions, and cultural organisations collaborate to stage the **final milking ceremony**, broadcast internationally. The milk is treated as rare and irreplaceable, auctioned to an exclusive circle of buyers. Multimillionaires, billionaires, museums, dairy cooperatives, powerful institutions, turning what was once an everyday commodity into a luxury artefact and cultural relic. 

The final glass goes to a billionaire whose spoiled children insist on flavouring the last milk on Earth to make it taste like orange juice.

Afterward, the cow is placed in a **cryogenic preservation chamber**, suspended between life and death, in the hope that she might one day be studied to reverse the mutation. Carefully maintained but no longer part of any living ecosystem, she becomes at once a biological specimen and a monument.

*The project draws attention to the normalisation of exploitation in industrial food systems, where animal bodies are engineered purely for output. It reflects on how access to "natural" food increasingly becomes a class privilege — and how easily ecological loss is turned into spectacle, ritual, and commodity, instead of a cause to address at its root.*
