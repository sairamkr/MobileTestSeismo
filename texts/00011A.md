







#11000
*By Mrudula*

**Inter-caste marriages are so rare and socially unsettling that society responded by turning them into frozen artefacts.**   
In this world, couples who cross caste boundaries are symbolically preserved inside an ice cube in a repository. Each cube carried a distinct colour, determined by the perceived “purity level” of the union; its hue depended on which castes had crossed paths.   
These cubes resembled flavoured ice treats, visually vibrant yet socially loaded. **People can approach the huge ice blocks, observe their colours, and even lick them to experience the taste of transgressions :** *the sweetness of love and companionship, the bitterness of stigma and judgment, and the rigid social boundaries that refuse to melt.* 

The cubes turned relationships into objects of spectacle, making the hierarchy visible. **In a society deeply invested in casteism and purity, these frozen forms functioned as markers, preserving what was seen as a transgression while also warning others about crossing the same boundaries.**

Seen from our present moment, this speculative world begins to feel unsettlingly familiar. Even today, while laws and social attitudes have shifted in many places, ideas of purity, honour, and suitability continue to influence how relationships are perceived. Honor killings that violently ordain who their children form relationships with, communities discouraging certain unions, and social pressure still determine which forms of love are celebrated and which are resisted. Many couples who challenge caste boundaries experience emotional isolation, strained family ties, or social distancing, forms of symbolic freezing that halt the natural flow of relationships. 

The speculation invites viewers to notice how easily systems of hierarchy have become implicit in everyday practices and traditions, so that we stop questioning them. What appears aesthetic, or harmless on the surface, may still carry deep structures of exclusion beneath it. 

Perhaps, this speculation ponders \- if we turn our prejudices into material objects, we might be able to question why certain relationships are still evaluated through inherited notions of purity and belonging.

**Rather than offering a distant fantasy where we solve this intractable problem, the frozen cubes reveal how society has long attempted to preserve and control social order by regulating intimacy itself by every new technological means available.**
